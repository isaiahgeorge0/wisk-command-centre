# WISK — Brand Identity

This document defines the brand identity, voice, and visual language of
WISK. It is the reference for all marketing copy, product microcopy,
and brand decisions. When something is written in the name of WISK,
it should be consistent with everything here.

---

## The meaning of WISK

WISK is an acronym:

| Letter | Pillar |
|--------|--------|
| **W** | Wisdom |
| **I** | Integrity |
| **S** | Strength |
| **K** | Knowledge |

These four pillars describe what WISK gives to the people who use it:

- **Wisdom** — informed decisions, not just data
- **Integrity** — trustworthy, ethical, transparent (especially in AI)
- **Strength** — built for ambition; can handle complexity
- **Knowledge** — connects to AI insights, learning over time

### The whisk metaphor

The literal whisk reinforces the concept. A whisk combines different
ingredients into something better than the sum of its parts. That is
exactly what WISK does — projects, tasks, leads, content, and goals are
nothing on their own. Whisked together, they become a business.

The metaphor should feel natural, not forced. Use it when it lands.
Never explain it to death.

---

## Brand pillars in product decisions

Every feature WISK ships should serve at least one of **W / I / S / K**.

| Pillar | What it covers in product |
|--------|--------------------------|
| **Wisdom** | AI digest, insights, recommendations, pattern recognition |
| **Integrity** | Privacy, security, encrypted tokens, RLS, explainable AI |
| **Strength** | Depth, capability, handling real complexity for ambitious users |
| **Knowledge** | Connections between sections, learning from user data over time |

When evaluating a new feature, ask: **which pillar does this strengthen?**
If the answer is none, the feature is probably not aligned with WISK.

## Voice and language

WISK speaks like a capable, trusted advisor. Not corporate. Not juvenile.
Confident but warm. The voice should feel like the smartest person in
your business — the one who notices what others miss.

### Avoid

- Generic SaaS marketing language ("powerful", "seamless", "robust")
- Over-promising AI capabilities
- Diminutive language — "just", "simple", "easy". WISK is for ambitious
  people who are not afraid of depth
- Jargon for its own sake

### Embrace

- The whisk metaphor when it lands naturally
- The acronym revealed in moments that matter
- Subtle wit ("whisking your data together…")
- Direct, specific language over vague benefits

## Where the brand shows up

### Marketing site

- **Hero** — primary headline ("Your business. Centralised.") plus a
  sub-line that reveals the acronym
- **"About WISK" section** — dedicated space explaining the meaning.
  Read once, referenced forever
- **Footer** — subtle acknowledgement of the acronym
- **404 page** — opportunity for warmth and brand personality

### Product

- **`/welcome` onboarding** — first-time users see the acronym revealed
  as part of the brand story. Four-word animation:
  Wisdom → Integrity → Strength → Knowledge → **WISK**
- **Loading states** — "whisking your data together…" type microcopy
- **Empty states** — small moments to reinforce brand voice
- **Error messages** — calm and capable; never blame the user

### Communications

- Email signatures
- Welcome emails for new users (post-invite approval)
- Changelog entries — written with personality, not just bullet points
- Feedback responses — voice consistent with the product tone

## Visual identity

The acronym lends itself to abstract visual treatments — things
converging, ingredients combining, a single point of synthesis. Avoid
literal kitchen-whisk imagery; it cheapens the concept.

### Current visual language

- Purple-to-teal gradient wordmark
- Dark mode default; light mode as a first-class alternate
- Clean typography, generous spacing
- Animation purposeful rather than decorative (200–400ms, Framer Motion)

The visual language should feel considered and intentional at every
scale — from the favicon to a full marketing hero.

## What WISK is not

- Not corporate
- Not playful at the expense of trust
- Not a productivity tool for the average person
- Not a feature factory
- Not generic

These "not" statements are as important as the positive ones. When a
brand decision feels off, check it against this list first.

---

## Long-term brand vision

WISK should feel like a category-defining product the moment someone
encounters it. Not "another Notion alternative." Not "Asana but for
creators." A genuinely new thing with a genuinely meaningful name.

The acronym is the foundation. Every brand moment should either reinforce
the four pillars or extend the whisk metaphor in a way that feels
natural — never laboured, never explained twice.

When the brand is working, users should feel that WISK understands them
and their ambition before they have had to explain it.
